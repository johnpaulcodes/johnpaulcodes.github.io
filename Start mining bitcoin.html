How to get started!

For this tutorial I will be keeping it simple and we will assume a residential mining operation of 200-amps or less in size. Bitcoin mining uses several useful every-day trade skills such as carpentry, plumbing, and electrician work to name a few. What skills are necessary depends on the complexity of your operation. To get started, you will have to become at the least knowledgeable across the following subjects to build your first operation without much headache. That being said, here are the things you will have to consider when implementing your first at-home mining operation. These are in order of significance (from my own personal opinion), though categorically, they are all equally important to understand.

  • Power Source

    • To get started, a majority of new miners will likely be using grid-power to fuel operations. Grid-power is essentially the network of power cables owned by the big utility providors (speaking of the USA-grid) that are underneath our feet and powering our homes. This "grid power" as it's called is the most accessible to us plebians as it has had the most time to be developed for public use, first documented in the 1880's in fact. The ease of access, however, also makes grid-power the most expensive to consume. Most rates in the US range between $0.10c per Kilowatthour in Utah up to $0.24c per Kilowatthour in Rhode Island. We could go off-topic and get into how utility companies are essentially monopolies controling all the power that 99% of us need to use to power our homes but that is for a different article.

  • Energy Consumption

    • Energy Consumption is traditionally measured in Kilowatthours. If we were to translate this explain this to a 5 year-old, Kilowatts can be seen as the speed of a car while the Hours can be seen as the distance the car has traveled. Lets say 1 KilowattHour is equal to 1mph. If a car is moving at 1 mile-per-hour, then we can assume that in 5 hours it will have traveled a total of 5 miles. Translating that to power consumption language, that would mean 1 Kilowatt of power used for 5 hours consumed a total of 5 KilowattHours of power which we will call the Total Power Consumed.

    To Calculate Costs, all we need to do is multiply Total Power Consmption by the energy rate your utility company has provided you. If you have a power rate of $0.10c per KilowattHour, you would simply multiply

    • $0.10c x Total Power Consumed = Total Power Cost

    In this case, our total cost would be $0.50c since 5 KilowattHours multiplied by $0.10c gives us, $0.50c. Knowing how to do this simple math is the building block to determining operating costs. These are operational costs that you will have no matter what circumstances and there is no way around it. You need power for these ASIC's to work. This does not include overhead costs such as rent payments or having a backup rental-generator if you end up going that route (I don't believe it's necessary). If you are going to do this in a leased home, you absolutely need to contact your landlord and make sure it is okay first. The chance of a fire is small if you do things the right way and don't overload things but of course you need to disclose things like this. Just a note.

  • Mining Pools & Profitability

    1. Revenues

      2. The way to calculate your revenue share of Bitcoin depends on your mining pool. AntPool, F2Pool, & ViaBTC have in common a "guaranteed" payout based on your contributed hashrate. Because the payouts are guaranteed, you will have to pay them a higher pool fee to participate. It is calculated as follows.

      • (Avg. Blocks per Day) x (Your hashrate / Pool hashrate) - Mining Pool Fees = Block Reward Payout
        •

      In certain circumstances, like with SlushPool, you are only paid if the pool solves a new block. Because of this, you pay SlushPool a smaller mining pool fee to compensate for the additional risk of potentially solving zero blocks in a day, though this is highly unlikely. We will use the Antminer S19j Pro for the example. Remember you can interchange these numbers with the MicroBT ASIC figures if you want.

      Until block 810,000, each block subsidizes miners with 6.25 Bitcoin plus transaction fees. For simplicity, we will not include transaction fees in our calculations since those always vary. Look at the table below to see potential payouts based on the Pool:

      Anyways, looking at SlushPool, 5 blocks mined in one day amounts to 31.25 BTC. At $49,000 USD per Bitcoin, this is equal to ~$1,500,000 USD total that will be split among the entire pool for the day. Remember that your reward out of that number is proportional to the hashrate you are contributing.

      If you contribute 110 T/h with a single ASIC to a total pool hashrate of 6,000,000 T/h (also known as 6 E/h or exahashes); that means you will receive 0.000018% of the total BTC mined that day in SlushPool. In this case

      • 0.000018% of 31.25 BTC = 0.00056250 BTC or 56,250 Satoshis

      Assuming Bitcoin is $49,000 USD; Our daily revenue in dollars is roughly estimated to be $27.60. Now that we have our projected top line revenue numbers, we have to reduce it and find our proforma net income.

    2. Electricity Expenses

      3. To calculate daily expenses on a single Antminer S19j Pro, we will have to determine how much power consumption it will use in 24 hours.

      One miner uses 3,250 Watts of electricity. If we multiply this number by 24 hours, we will have 78,000 WattHours. We need to convert this to KilowattHours by dividing it by 1,000 because there is 1,000 Watts in 1 Killowatt of power. In total we consumed 78 KilowattHours of power for the day which still needs to be converted to dollars.

      78 KWh multiplied by the electricity rate of $0.10c per KWh means I would have spent $7.80 USD to operate my ASIC for one day.

    3. Overhead Expenses (Situational)

      4. This will vary case-by-case but if you are planning on mining in a landlords property or renting a small warehouse to start mining Bitcoin, you need to consider the cyclical nature of Bitcoin's market value. If by chance the price of Bitcoin drops to unprofitable levels, you will need to shut down your operations. The problem if this were to happen is.. well.. you ain't makin' no moneys. So if you would like to go this route, you absolutely have to be sure you have funds on the side to cover your ass in the event of a bear market where you can't keep your machines on. Luckily, we can figure out the breakeven-price for Bitcoin to figure out at what point we need to shut down.

    4. Breakeven Bitcoin Price

      5. In order to calculate our break even price, we must add up all of our fixed costs into a total and divide it by the total number of Bitcoin mined. You can do this either per-day or per-month, it's completely your preference as the math will still check out.

      Let's pull the daily operational costs of $7.80 in electricity and multiply that by 31 days. If you want to pay your rent with your Bitcoin (if you have a lease), factor your payment into the equation. To start, lets not assume any kind of rent payment. Looking at electricity costs only, $242 per month is your monthly obligation no matter what happens. Since we already have a rough idea of how much Bitcoin we will mine every month, and we know how much our expense is every month, we can find out the break-even price for Bitcoin before we have to turn things off.

      We projected earning roughly 1,743,000 Satoshis each month or 0.01743 BTC. This comes out to $854 USD revenue in one month if you sold 100% of it. Our actual cost for this Bitcoin was only $242. This could also be seen as purchasing these 1.74 million Satoshis for $242. If we use the following equation below

      • Electricity Cost / Bitcoin Mined = Bitcoin Cost-Basis (aka Break Even Price)
        •

      Using this forumula, that means our cost basis for Bitcoin is actualy only $13,844 per Bitcoin. We can look at it as we are buying Bitcoin for this price. The difference is instead of buying it from the open-market at the prevailing market rate, we are instead paying the energy providor while Bitcoin as a protocol subsizes us directly. This $13,844 number is the price at which if Bitcoin fell to, you would need to shut down operations. At this Bitcoin price and below, it is cheaper to acquire Bitcoin by purchasing it from the open-market rather than mining it yourself.

      Now lets assume you have a rent payment that you would like to cover 100% of with your mining operation. All you need to do is add your rent payment to your electricity cost per month and you will find the new adjusted-cost basis to cover your rent. Let's assume rent is $1,000 and the electricity rate is the exact same.
      Instead of looking at it as a "break-even", this new figure would tell you what price Bitcoin would need to be in order to cover your electricity costs and also cover 100% of your rent payment. The adjusted cost basis for this example is now $71,250 per Bitcoin in order to cover both electricity costs and a rent payment. Also remember that this is assuming you have a hashrate of 110 T/h at today's mining difficulty.

      What you decide to calculate in your cost basis is strictly based upon your existing income if you have any already and your end goals. While the break-even price includes only Bitcoin's price, it does not include changes in Bitcoin mining difficulty which will reduce your reward payouts.

  • Mining Difficulty

    • If you've made it this far, you're probably aware that Bitcoin's supply issuance is totally inelastic, meaning that it cannot be changed. No matter how many people decide to start mining Bitcoin, the amount of Bitcoin issued every day is the exact same. Unlike gold, a rise in price does not mean more Bitcoin. The same goes if Bitcoin's price decides to fall. You must factor in expected mining difficulty changes to get a more accurate reading of profitability.

    The way you do this is simply putting a figure of how much more new hashrate will come online per month. On average, it is somewhere close to 4% per month. Using the rule-of-72, we can predict that our reward will be cut in half every 18-months. This means if Bitcoin's price has not doubled after 18 months, mining difficulty increased 4% per month over that period of time, and we did not increase our own hashrate, then we can assume we will earn half of what we did in the first month of mining.   • Noise

    • One thing ASICs are notorious for is the noise their fans produce to cool their processing chips. The faster the fans are spinning, the louder they will sound. Compared to the previous Antminer Generation, the Antminer S9, the newer Antminer S19j Pro's have double the fans for double the noise! So what is a good way to combat this noise, especially from the comfort of your home and without annoying your neighbors 24-hours, 7-days a week?

    There are two different ways to treat sound. The two methods are:

    • Sound Acoustics Treatment
      •
    • Sound isolation
      •

    Sound acoustics treatment is more so treating how the sound bounces while sound isolation is completely isolating sound to a specific area without leaks. This sound isolation is what we will be focusing on as Bitcoin miners.

    Sound can be compared to the water in a fish tank. You don't want any to leak right? So consider how fish tanks are designed to hold all the water. It's sealed, heavy, and non-pourous. Now imagine trying to make a fish tank with blankets and a lot of sponges instead.. it wouldn't work very well would it? The same concept applies to sound isolation. What you are looking for in making these ASICs quiet is mass.

    Without mass, you can consider all of your efforts to make it quieter a total waste of time. If you want your custom enclosure that will hold your ASIC to reduce the noise produced by these fans, you will need to make it a little bit heavy and massful. Consider using things like mass-loaded vinyls and hardwood when building your ASIC enclosure. I will include several photo examples and several resources for you to read further on this here. This is the simplest explanation I can give to see real results in isolation noise coming from these devices.

  • Heat

    • Heat is an important component in the efficiency of your ASICs. While the chips love to be hot, you can't allow them to get too hot. Generally, the easiest way to take care of heat is to just create some kind of ventilation duct for your ASIC where any hot air is immediately led outdoors with no chance for the hot air to recirculate back into the intake fan. It is important that you do not let much if any hot air recirulate into the intake fans, especially if you decide to build an enclosure around it. The feedback loop of hot air will ruin the performance of your mining equipment and thus reducing the potential output of your operation. It's also important that you make sure the air around the ASIC is not too dirty or dusty. The bane of all computers in existence is heat and dust, so if you can manage to build an enclosure that channels heat out and also channels new clean air in, you will have far less maintenance on your ASICs to deal with. Take the time to figure out what kind of design you want to go with for your application. Maybe you have an extra garage you can place your ASICs inside of or possibly a spare room in your house that is empty all the time and if you were to have a guest you can just turn things off for a couple nights.

    Another note: Too cold is also very bad! This is actually worse for your ASICs than the hot air because cold temperatures will produce condensation and moisture is very very very bad for these devices. Any kind of water build up could result in rust forming on your hashboards so it is crucial that you make sure you have some kind of backup heat generation if you happen to live in a colder than usual climate. If this is your case, it isn't a terrible idea to recirculate some of that hot air coming out of the exhaust back into the ASIC to keep things warm. This is the only situation that this is considered normal to do.
